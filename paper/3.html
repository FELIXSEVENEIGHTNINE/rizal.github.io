If Noli Me Tangere was a call for reform, El Filibusterismo (The Reign of Greed) serves as a warning about the consequences of failed reform. Published in 1891, the novel adopts a darker tone, delving into themes of revolution, revenge, and the moral complexities of combating oppression. The character of Simoun, a wealthy jeweler who secretly plots to overthrow the Spanish regime, represents the dangers of unchecked anger and the futility of revenge. His failed revolution stands as a cautionary tale, emphasizing the need to address the root causes of injustice rather than merely seeking to destroy the oppressors.

The novel’s exploration of systemic corruption and inequality continues to resonate in a Philippines still grappling with political dynasties and economic disparity. Simoun’s tragic fate serves as a reminder that violence and revenge cannot solve societal problems; true change requires systemic reform and collective action. Rizal’s nuanced portrayal of revolution challenges us to reflect on the ethics of activism and the importance of moral integrity in the fight for justice.

El Filibusterismo is a powerful reminder that the path to change is fraught with challenges, but it is a path we must walk with courage, wisdom, and a commitment to the common good.

Rizal’s novels did more than expose the injustices of colonial rule; they were instrumental in shaping Filipino identity and inspiring the movement for independence. By writing in Spanish and addressing both Filipino and international audiences, Rizal ensured that the plight of his people would not go unnoticed.

Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo sparked a sense of national consciousness among Filipinos, uniting them in their shared desire for freedom. These novels inspired the Propaganda Movement and later the Philippine Revolution, demonstrating the power of literature as a tool for social change.

Rizal’s works continue to be studied in schools, serving as a reminder to new generations about the importance of critical thinking, social responsibility, and patriotism. The themes of his novels—corruption, inequality, and the fight for justice—remain relevant in contemporary discussions about governance and human rights. Rizal’s literary masterpieces are not merely relics of the past; they are living testaments to the power of ideas and the enduring spirit of the Filipino people.

While Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo are José Rizal's most famous works, his lesser-known writings also provide valuable insights into his intellectual brilliance and his vision for the Philippines. One such work, Mi Ultimo Adios ("My Last Farewell"), written on the eve of his execution, poignantly expresses Rizal’s profound love for his country and his readiness to sacrifice his life for its freedom. In The Indolence of the Filipinos, Rizal challenges the stereotype of Filipino laziness, arguing that colonial oppression and the lack of opportunities were the real causes of stagnation. In To the Young Women of Malolos, a letter celebrating the courage of women who fought for their right to education, Rizal highlights his progressive views on gender equality. Together, these works demonstrate Rizal's versatility as a writer and his unwavering commitment to addressing the social, political, and cultural issues of his time.
